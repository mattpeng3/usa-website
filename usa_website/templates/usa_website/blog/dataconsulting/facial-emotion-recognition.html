Facial Emotion Recognition

PM: Zhongling Jiang


Introduction

During the fall semester of 2017, Facial Emotion Team at Statistics Undergraduate Student Association (SUSA) to classify facial emotions from pictures.

Our project was to detect facial emotions from a dataset of 28 thousand images taken from around the internet according to 7 categories. We were classifying anger, neutral, sad, happy, surprised, disgust, and fear. The dataset we were using actually came from a Kaggle competition in 2013, as a part of Ian Goodfellow’s ICML workshop. That competition was ranked according to the highest categorization accuracy. The top validation score was 71.1% accuracy.

To be in the top 10, a competitor had to get an accuracy of 62%. To get in the top 25, one had to get above 50% accuracy. What we soon learned was that this high level of difficulty was due to a poor labelling of the data. Many pictures seemed to have ambiguous and almost objectively wrong labels, as if there was not a clear standard between annotators. Some of the pictures had weird markings on them, like watermarks from stock photos, and some faces were at strange angles with important features cropped out.

Our first attempt to crack this was to use a basic 6-layer convolutional neural net (4 convolutional layers, 2 fully-connected). While training accuracy improved up to 70%, the validation accuracy stagnated in the low 50s. While I was watching the model train, I mistook the fast learning as an indication that we could breeze past 71% validation accuracy in the next 2 months.

We spent about a month on trying different methods. The team split in two, in pursuit of two separate ideas. The first half, tried out pre-trained models on the data. I led the other half in pursuit of a new idea.


Pre-trained Models

Trying out pre-trained models on any Kaggle competition is supposed to provide the baseline estimate of how deep learning models will do on that data. However, our results were sub-baseline. Our attempts to train the dense layers of the pre-trained models flopped. Both train and validation accuracy were in the low 30s.

This was probably due to the image data augmentation process that we used. We used the augmentation process implemented in the standard Keras packaging when training the dense layers on the pre-trained models. However, our models seemed to flatline around 25% accuracy every time. We noticed the same behavior with vanilla CNN models trained on the data as well.

Further investigation of the augmentation process revealed that we mistakenly used a resize function that tiled the photos, as opposed to resizing them. (This was an error from using Numpy’s resize function instead of CV2’s resize function.) And some of the augmentation processes we had used manipulated images beyond our recognition. The final models were trained without the data augmentation process. However, these models were too large to be effectively trained on our AWS EC2 instance, and we did not leave enough time for them to converge properly.


Explicitly Overfitting Models

This idea was actually one inspired from a paper written by Master Kaggler Mikel Bober-Irizar. If you’ve read some of my other posts, you may have seen his name before. The gist of his paper, Cultivating DNN Diversity for Large Scale Video Labeling, is that overfit models can learn different patterns in the training data that can be combined with the unique patterns of other overfit models to produce a better global model. Hence, I thought it would be worthwhile to overfit a few (8) neural nets on the data. To “cultivate diversity” we trained 4 models of a standard convolutional architecture and 4 models using ResNet18’s architecture. My teammate and I wrote a bash script to loop through the model creation and training process until the training accuracy flatlined or 50 epochs were reached. Here’s the link to the code.

At first, we only let the models train for 7–15 epochs on the data, and that produced surprisingly good results. We tried a few different ways of combining these initial models, using logistic regression and fully-connected neural nets on the output vectors. Specifically, we tried logistic regression classifiers and 2 fully connected layers with a softmax on the bottleneck features of the (4) CNNs and the (4) ResNet18 architectures. We even tried piping the same images to another CNN which operated on the bottleneck features of the ensemble model, but that failed to converge to validation accuracy above 50%.

However, the best method was just to sum their prediction vectors and predict the class with the highest summed probability. We present our final ensemble architecture below.

It took around 24 hours to train all 8 models on a single AWS EC2 p2 instance. We would have liked to train the models for longer, however both the convolutional and residual architectures seemed to not improve their training accuracy beyond a few hours. The final ensemble model got us a decent 65% validation accuracy which puts us in the top 5 competitors.
